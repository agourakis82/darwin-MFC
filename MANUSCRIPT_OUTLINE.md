## 3. Abstract Structure

### Background

Emergency digital tools often provide content access but insufficient workflow support under interruption.

### Objective

To design and evaluate a workflow-centered acute care interface that supports persistent patient context, inline decision support, interruption recovery, and structured handoff.

### Methods

Simulation-based within-subject comparison between the current and redesigned interface across sentinel emergency workflows.

### Results

Report primary and secondary endpoints:
- time to first useful action
- time to dose support
- omitted steps
- interruption recovery
- usability
- workload

### Conclusions

A case-centered, workflow-first emergency interface may improve task performance and continuity without increasing burden.

## 7. Discussion Outline

### Main finding

The redesign improved workflow continuity and reduced interruption cost.

### Interpretation

The main benefit came from workflow support, not simply visual polish.

### Relationship to prior literature

Connect to:
- emergency EHR usability
- cognitive aids
- situational awareness displays
- alert fatigue
- handoff literature

### Clinical relevance

Potential value for bedside protocol execution and training.

### Design implications

Why case-centered state and inline support matter more than content breadth.

### Limitations

- simulation only
- limited participant pool
- sentinel workflows only
- no live clinical deployment

### Future work

- live clinical testing
- role-board validation
- AI-assisted summary with human review
